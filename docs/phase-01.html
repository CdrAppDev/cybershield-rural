Chapter 1 — The Opportunity

$200 million to South Carolina with explicit cybersecurity eligibility

The CMS Rural Health Transformation Program allocated $200,030,252 to South Carolina with explicit cybersecurity eligibility language under Category F: Technology and Cybersecurity Modernization (CMS, December 2025). The program represents the largest federal investment in rural health infrastructure in over a decade. No venture-backed startup dedicated exclusively to rural hospital cybersecurity was identified during the 2024–2025 research period (Capital thesis research, January 2026). This combination—a new, large funding stream explicitly naming cybersecurity, and no existing startup positioned to capture it—defines the capital opportunity.

Chapter 2 — The Evidence

$19.3 billion market, $18 billion in VC investment, and a state-level cybersecurity precedent

The healthcare cybersecurity market was valued at $19.3 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach $75 billion by 2032 at an 18.5% CAGR (Fortune Business Insights, 2024). Total cybersecurity venture capital investment reached $18 billion in 2025, up 52% year-over-year (Pinpoint Search Group, January 2026). At the state level, SCRA invested $250,000 equity plus a $50,000 grant in ThreatCaptain, a cybersecurity startup, and launched a Center for Cybersecurity in December 2025 (SCRA, December 2025). These data points establish that capital is flowing to both healthcare cybersecurity and South Carolina-based cybersecurity ventures.

Chapter 3 — The Constraints

Six constraints bound the funding landscape

The capital thesis identifies six material constraints. First, RHT subaward criteria have not yet been published by SCDHHS; all RHT assessments are based on federal program language, not state implementation details (CMS, December 2025). Second, SBIR/STTR programs expired on September 30, 2025, pending Congressional reauthorization (SBIR.gov, 2025). Third, SCRA membership requires a working product beyond proof-of-concept (SC Launch, 2025). Fourth, all VC paths require demonstrated traction. Fifth, ARPA-H viability is uncertain following reported funding cuts. Sixth, seven viable funders operate on unknown or rolling timelines, limiting planning precision.

Section 03 / Funder Deep Dives

Funder Deep Dives

Detailed analysis of the seven highest-priority funding paths, including alignment strengths, gaps, and timeline considerations.

CMS Rural Health Transformation

Strong
▼

The CMS Rural Health Transformation Program is a $50 billion, five-year federal initiative representing the largest investment in rural health infrastructure in over a decade. South Carolina received $200,030,252 in allocations. Category F explicitly names Technology and Cybersecurity Modernization as an eligible investment area. The Tech Catalyst Fund caps individual startup investments at $20M per year. (CMS, December 2025)

Key Criteria

  • Must be a subrecipient through SCDHHS (state Medicaid agency administers funds)
  • Category F: Technology and Cybersecurity Modernization eligible
  • Tech Catalyst Fund: capped at $20M/year for startup investments
  • Requires demonstrated alignment with rural health outcomes
  • Sustainability plan required beyond grant period

Alignment Strengths

  • Explicit cybersecurity language in Category F eligibility criteria
  • $200M allocation to South Carolina specifically
  • Grant-based: no equity dilution
  • Multi-year funding provides runway stability

Alignment Gaps

  • Subaward criteria not yet published by SCDHHS (blocking)
  • Needs rural hospital partner as subrecipient sponsor (significant)
  • Sustainability plan required beyond grant period (significant)

Timeline: Subaward criteria expected 2026. No fixed application deadline published.

SCRA / SC Launch Inc.

Strong
▼

SCRA operates SC Launch, a $15 million fund that has generated $2.9 billion in follow-on capital since 2006. In February 2025, SCRA invested $250,000 equity plus a $50,000 grant in ThreatCaptain, a cybersecurity startup. In December 2025, SCRA launched a Center for Cybersecurity, signaling strategic priority for the sector. No healthcare cybersecurity company is currently in the SCRA portfolio. (SCRA, 2025)

Key Criteria

  • SC Launch membership requires working product beyond proof-of-concept
  • Investment range: $25K–$550K (grants and equity)
  • Company must be incorporated in or relocating to South Carolina
  • Acceptance rate below 40%

Alignment Strengths

  • Demonstrated cybersecurity investment precedent (ThreatCaptain)
  • Center for Cybersecurity launched December 2025
  • No healthcare cybersecurity company in current portfolio
  • $2.9B follow-on capital track record signals credibility to later investors

Alignment Gaps

  • Requires working product beyond POC (blocking)
  • Below 40% acceptance rate (significant)

Timeline: Rolling applications. Product readiness is the gating factor.

Town Hall Ventures

Strong
▼

Town Hall Ventures invests $1M–$25M across stages in companies serving underserved populations. The firm was co-founded by Andy Slavitt, former Acting Administrator of CMS, providing deep federal healthcare policy expertise. The fund thesis explicitly targets healthcare access for rural and underserved communities. (Town Hall Ventures, 2025)

Key Criteria

  • Companies serving underserved populations, including rural communities
  • Healthcare and government-adjacent technology
  • Investment range: $1M–$25M across stages

Alignment Strengths

  • Explicit focus on underserved populations including rural
  • Former CMS Administrator as co-founder (federal alignment)
  • Flexible stage and check size

Alignment Gaps

  • Requires demonstrated traction (significant)

Timeline: Rolling. Traction required before engagement.

CommonSpirit Ventures

Strong
▼

CommonSpirit Health Ventures is a $250 million strategic venture fund backed by CommonSpirit Health, one of the largest nonprofit health systems in the United States. The fund has made 36 investments to date. CommonSpirit operates Critical Access Hospitals in 21 states, providing a potential pathway to pilot deployment and customer acquisition. (CommonSpirit Ventures, 2025)

Key Criteria

  • Strategic alignment with CommonSpirit Health system needs
  • Healthcare technology focus
  • Amount not publicly disclosed per deal

Alignment Strengths

  • Operates Critical Access Hospitals in 21 states (built-in customer base)
  • $250M fund with 36 investments (active deployer)
  • Strategic investor: can provide both capital and customers

Alignment Gaps

  • No cybersecurity investments identified in current portfolio (significant)
  • Requires product maturity for strategic evaluation (significant)

Timeline: Rolling. Product maturity and strategic fit drive timing.

ARPA-H UPGRADE/DIGIHEALS

Moderate
▼

ARPA-H has deployed over $100 million through the UPGRADE and DIGIHEALS programs targeting healthcare cybersecurity. Individual awards range from $9.5M to $19M. The programs fund research and development of novel cybersecurity tools for healthcare infrastructure. However, ARPA-H has faced reported funding cuts, and the programs have historically favored academic and established research institutions. (ARPA-H, 2024–2025)

Key Criteria

  • Novel R&D approach to healthcare cybersecurity
  • Award range: $9.5M–$19M per project
  • Open to commercial entities, academic institutions, and nonprofits

Alignment Strengths

  • Large award sizes ($9.5M–$19M) provide substantial runway
  • Directly targets healthcare cybersecurity
  • Non-dilutive grant funding

Alignment Gaps

  • R&D requirements may not align with product-stage company (blocking)
  • Funding cuts introduce viability uncertainty (blocking)
  • Historical bias toward academic and established institutions (significant)

Timeline: Program status uncertain due to reported funding cuts. Monitor only.

Venture Capital Cluster

Moderate
▼

Four venture capital firms with relevant thesis overlap were identified. Flare Capital Partners ($1B AUM) invested in ClearDATA, a healthcare cloud security company, establishing healthcare cybersecurity precedent. Ballistic Ventures ($600M+ fund) is a cybersecurity-only firm investing at Seed and Series A. Ten Eleven Ventures ($1B+ AUM) invested in Anatomy IT, a healthcare-focused managed services provider. Cultivation Capital operates dual cybersecurity and healthcare funds ($100K–$3.5M checks). All four require demonstrated traction before engagement.

Key Criteria

  • Flare Capital: $1M–$8M, healthcare IT focus, Seed through Series B
  • Ballistic Ventures: Cybersecurity-only, Seed and Series A, operator-founded
  • Ten Eleven Ventures: Cybersecurity-focused, stage agnostic, $1B+ under management
  • Cultivation Capital: $100K–$3.5M, dual cybersecurity + healthcare funds, Seed to Series A

Alignment Strengths

  • Portfolio precedents in healthcare cybersecurity (Flare: ClearDATA; Ten Eleven: Anatomy IT)
  • Cybersecurity-dedicated funds (Ballistic, Ten Eleven) understand the sector
  • Cultivation Capital uniquely spans both cybersecurity and healthcare

Alignment Gaps

  • All require demonstrated traction: revenue, customers, or LOIs (significant)
  • Rolling processes with no fixed deadlines; timing uncertain (significant)

Timeline: All operate rolling processes. Engagement appropriate post-Phase 12 (customer traction).

Accelerators

Strong
▼

Three accelerators offer distinct value propositions for an early-stage healthcare cybersecurity venture. MassChallenge is a zero-equity accelerator offering prize funding and dual-track eligibility (HealthTech and Cybersecurity). MACH37 is a cybersecurity-focused accelerator with 94 graduates, 83% company survival rate, 64% funded rate, and takes 5% equity. Health Wildcatters is a healthcare-focused accelerator offering $30K–$380K for 8% equity, ranked among top healthcare accelerators nationally.

Key Criteria

  • MassChallenge: Zero equity, prizes up to $100K+, dual-track HealthTech + Cybersecurity, application deadline April 2026
  • MACH37: 5% equity, cybersecurity-focused, 90-day program, Virginia-based
  • Health Wildcatters: $30K–$380K for 8% equity, healthcare-focused, Dallas-based

Alignment Strengths

  • MassChallenge: zero equity model preserves ownership; dual-track eligibility
  • MACH37: 83% survival rate and 64% funded rate demonstrate track record
  • Health Wildcatters: healthcare domain expertise and top-ranked program
  • All three accept early-stage companies

Alignment Gaps

  • MACH37 and Health Wildcatters require equity (5% and 8% respectively)
  • Geographic requirements may apply (MACH37: Virginia; Health Wildcatters: Dallas)

Timeline: MassChallenge application deadline April 2026. MACH37 and Health Wildcatters operate cohort-based cycles.

Which funding track to prioritize?
Strategic

Revisit after Phase 06 — Business Thesis will model revenue scenarios against each funding track.

Three tracks offer distinct risk/reward profiles. Grant-first (RHT + SCRA) provides non-dilutive capital but depends on unpublished criteria and product readiness. Accelerator-first (MassChallenge/MACH37) provides structure, mentorship, and credibility but may require relocation. Parallel approach maximizes optionality but increases operational complexity.

Grant-first (RHT + SCRA) → Non-dilutive path. Requires SCDHHS criteria publication and working product. Highest potential award sizes.
Accelerator-first (MassChallenge/MACH37) → Structured path. MassChallenge zero-equity model preserves ownership. MACH37 provides cybersecurity network. Earliest possible entry: April 2026 application.
Parallel approach → Apply to accelerators while monitoring RHT criteria and building product for SCRA. Maximizes optionality at cost of focus.

Strategic

The academic bias in historical awards is a significant but not blocking concern.

Deprioritize → Focus on grant and accelerator tracks with clearer paths. Revisit if ARPA-H stabilizes.
Monitor only → Track ARPA-H program status without investing preparation effort. Re-evaluate at Phase 05.

When to approach VC investors?
Strategic

Revisit after Phase 12 — Customer Traction will determine readiness for VC engagement.

All four identified VC firms (Flare Capital, Ballistic Ventures, Ten Eleven Ventures, Cultivation Capital) require demonstrated traction. Total cybersecurity VC investment reached $18B in 2025 (up 52% YoY), indicating strong market appetite. Premature VC engagement risks burning relationships without adequate proof points.

Post-Phase 12 → Approach VCs with customer traction (LOIs, revenue, or pilot data). Standard path: accelerator graduation or SCRA validation as credibility signal.
